Introduction

Overview

GLP-1 receptor agonists like semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro) have revolutionized treatment for type 2 diabetes and obesity. However, the molecular mechanisms underlying their therapeutic effects remain incompletely understood. This project investigates how GLP-1 receptor (GLP-1R) signaling affects gene expression in metabolically active tissues using RNA-seq data from mice treated with a dual GLP-1R/GIPR agonist (mGIPR-Ab/P1). Our analysis revealed tissue-specific responses, unexpected GIPR dominance in regulating peripheral gene expression, and modest transcriptional changes in the predicted cAMP/PKA pathway.

Objective

This project aims to characterize the transcriptional response to GLP-1R signaling in liver and adipose tissue using published RNA-seq data from GEO (GSE293291). The dataset includes 60 samples across 4 genotypes, 2 treatment conditions, and 2 tissues.

Specific objectives include:

  1. Test the hypothesis that GLP-1R activation increases expression of cAMP/PKA pathway genes (PRKACA, PRKACB, CREB1, etc.)
  2. Compare treatment effects (mGIPR-Ab/P1 dual agonist drug) with receptor knockout models (CNS-specific GLP-1R knockout and GIPR knockout) to distinguish direct receptor-mediated effects
  3. Evaluate tissue-specific responses between liver and inguinal white adipose tissue (iWAT)
  4. Assess the relative importance of GIPR versus GLP-1R in regulating peripheral gene expression through CNS-mediated pathways
  5. Identify differentially expressed genes that may contribute to the metabolic benefits of incretin-based therapies

Research Question and Hypothesis

Research Question:
Do cells that express GLP-1R show increased expression of downstream cAMP/PKA pathway genes in response to receptor activation?

Hypothesis:
We predicted that treatment with the mGIPR-Ab/P1 dual agonist would strongly upregulate GLP-1R expression and activate downstream cAMP/PKA pathway genes in both liver and adipose tissue, with similar effects across both tissues.

Independent Variable: Treatment condition (vehicle control vs. mGIPR-Ab/P1 dual agonist)

Background Knowledge on GLP-1

GLP-1 (glucagon-like peptide-1) is an incretin hormone secreted by intestinal L-cells in response to food intake. It plays a central role in glucose homeostasis and energy regulation by:

  • Enhancing insulin secretion from pancreatic beta cells in a glucose-dependent manner
  • Suppressing glucagon release from alpha cells
  • Slowing gastric emptying to reduce post-meal glucose spikes
  • Promoting satiety through central nervous system pathways

The GLP-1 receptor is a G-protein coupled receptor (GPCR) expressed in multiple tissues including pancreas, brain, heart, kidney, liver, and adipose tissue. Upon ligand binding, GLP-1R activates the cAMP/PKA signaling pathway, which drives downstream transcriptional changes. This pathway includes key genes such as PRKACA, PRKACB, and CREB1 that regulate metabolic gene expression.

Gap in Knowledge

While GLP-1 drugs are clinically effective, several questions remain unanswered:

  1. Which genes are directly regulated by GLP-1R signaling in peripheral metabolic tissues like liver and fat?
  2. Does GLP-1R activation robustly upregulate the cAMP/PKA pathway genes at the transcriptional level, or does it work through post-translational mechanisms?
  3. How does tissue-specific gene expression differ between liver and adipose tissue in response to GLP-1 treatment?
  4. What is the relative contribution of GIPR (glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide receptor), which is often co-targeted with GLP-1R in dual agonist drugs?
  5. Do receptor knockout models reveal compensatory mechanisms or confirm direct receptor-mediated transcriptional effects?

Understanding these mechanisms could inform the development of more targeted therapies and explain why dual GLP-1R/GIPR agonists show enhanced efficacy compared to single-receptor drugs.

What is GLP-1R?

GLP-1R (Glucagon-Like Peptide-1 Receptor) is a class B GPCR that mediates the physiological effects of GLP-1 hormone. Key characteristics include:

  • Tissue distribution: Pancreatic islets, brain (hypothalamus, brainstem), heart, liver, adipose tissue, kidney
  • Signaling mechanism: Primarily through Gαs protein → adenylyl cyclase → cAMP → PKA activation → CREB phosphorylation → gene transcription
  • Therapeutic targeting: GLP-1R agonists (exenatide, liraglutide, semaglutide) are FDA-approved for diabetes and weight management
  • CNS-mediated effects: Central GLP-1R activation contributes to appetite suppression and energy expenditure regulation

GIPR (Glucose-Dependent Insulinotropic Polypeptide Receptor) is a related incretin receptor that responds to GIP hormone. It shares similar downstream signaling pathways and tissue distribution with GLP-1R. Dual GLP-1R/GIPR agonists like tirzepatide show superior efficacy compared to GLP-1R-only drugs, suggesting complementary or synergistic mechanisms.

The mGIPR-Ab/P1 agonist used in this study is a dual agonist that activates both GLP-1R and GIPR, allowing us to assess combined incretin signaling effects.

The GIP Receptor and Its Unexpected Role

While our project began with a focus on GLP-1R signaling, the glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide receptor (GIPR) emerged as a critically important and unexpected finding throughout our analysis.

What is GIPR?

The GIP receptor is a G protein-coupled receptor that binds glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide (GIP), the other major incretin hormone alongside GLP-1. Like GLP-1R, GIPR is expressed in multiple tissues including the pancreas, brain, adipose tissue, and liver. Upon activation, GIPR also signals through the cAMP/PKA pathway, stimulating insulin secretion and influencing energy metabolism.

Historically GIP was considered less therapeutically relevant than GLP-1 because early studies suggested that GIP-stimulated insulin secretion was impaired in people with type 2 diabetes. However more recent research has revealed that GIPR plays important roles in fat metabolism, bone density, and energy balance that go beyond its incretin function.

GIPR in the Brain

Both GLP-1R and GIPR are expressed in the central nervous system, particularly in regions involved in appetite regulation and energy homeostasis such as the hypothalamus and brainstem. CNS-specific receptor signaling has been shown to influence peripheral metabolic outcomes through neural circuits that communicate with peripheral organs including the liver and adipose tissue.

Our project used a CNS-specific GIPR knockout model (Gipr Syn-/-) where the GIP receptor was deleted specifically from synapsin-expressing neurons in the brain. This allowed us to isolate the contribution of brain GIPR signaling to peripheral gene expression without affecting GIPR in other tissues.

What Our Data Showed

Our GIPR knockout comparison produced the most striking result of the entire project. When we compared Gipr Syn-/- mice against Gipr fl/fl controls in liver tissue, we found 299 upregulated and 161 downregulated genes — far more than any other comparison including the drug treatment itself and the GLP-1R knockout.

This was unexpected for two reasons. First, our original hypothesis focused primarily on GLP-1R as the main driver of downstream signaling. Second, the magnitude of the GIPR effect in liver was larger than the GLP-1R effect, suggesting that CNS GIPR signaling may play a broader regulatory role in peripheral liver gene expression than previously appreciated.

What This Means

This finding points to what may be an important brain-liver axis mediated through GIPR. The brain appears to use the GIP receptor to regulate gene expression in peripheral tissues like the liver, and this regulation is substantial enough to produce more transcriptional changes than pharmacological activation of GLP-1R.

This does not contradict the established role of GLP-1R in metabolic regulation. Rather it suggests that the two receptor systems work in parallel and may have complementary roles — GLP-1R signaling may be more important for acute metabolic responses while GIPR in the CNS may contribute to baseline transcriptional regulation of peripheral tissues.

Implications for Dual Agonist Therapy

The drug used in our study, mGIPR-Ab/P1, is a dual-acting compound that blocks GIPR while activating GLP-1R. Our finding that GIPR deletion causes large transcriptional changes in liver raises an interesting question about how this dual mechanism contributes to the drug’s overall therapeutic effect. Future research should examine whether the transcriptional changes we observed in the GIPR knockout overlap with changes caused by the drug, which could help clarify the specific contribution of GIPR blockade to the drug’s metabolic benefits.
